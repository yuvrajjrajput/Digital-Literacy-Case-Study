# Task 5 – Cybercrime Case Study

## Crime Type: Phishing

---

## Case Study – "The Fake University Portal"

### Background

Phishing is one of the most common cybercrimes targeting college students in India.
It involves a criminal impersonating a trusted organisation — such as a bank, university,
or government body — to trick victims into revealing sensitive information like
passwords, OTPs, or bank details.

### How It Happens (Step by Step)

1. A first-year student receives an email that appears to be from their university's
   IT department. The email logo, fonts, and layout look identical to official
   communications.

2. The email states: *"Your student portal account will be deactivated in 24 hours.
   Click here to verify your credentials immediately."*

3. The student clicks the link, which opens a webpage that looks exactly like the
   official college login page — but the URL is slightly different
   (e.g., `vitbhopal-portal.info` instead of `vitbhopal.ac.in`).

4. The student enters their username and password. The fake page records these
   details and quietly sends them to the attacker.

5. The attacker now has full access to the student's college account, email, and
   potentially linked services. In some cases, the attacker finds saved payment
   details and makes unauthorised transactions.

### Who Is Usually Targeted

- First-year students who are unfamiliar with official communication formats
- Students who are anxious about account access or exam registrations
- Anyone who receives an urgent or threatening message and acts quickly without verifying

### Consequences

- Loss of access to academic accounts and email
- Potential financial loss if payment details were stored
- Identity theft using student ID details
- Psychological stress and disruption to academic performance
- In severe cases, the stolen account may be used for further fraud

### Source

This is a realistic fictional scenario based on common phishing patterns reported
by the [National Cyber Crime Portal of India](cybercrime.gov.in).

## Reflection

While researching phishing, the thing that surprised me most was how convincing
modern phishing emails and websites can look. Attackers put significant effort into
copying logos, layouts, and even email addresses so that the fake communication is
nearly indistinguishable from the real one. I had assumed that phishing attempts
were always obvious, but that is clearly not the case.

One habit I will personally change as a result of this research is always checking
the full URL of any website before entering my login details. I will also stop
clicking links directly from emails and instead navigate to websites manually by
typing the address in the browser. These small habits can prevent a large number
of phishing attacks.
